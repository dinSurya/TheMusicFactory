Concepts

Notes and Rests

Notes

Notes are symbols that indicate the pitch and the duration. There are several different symbols that represent different durations.

Rests

Rests are symbols that tell a musician to stop playing their instrument or singing. Like notes, ther have different symbols that indicate the duration of the rest.

Parts of the Staff

The staff is a set of lines and spaces that can be used to read music. Every staff has 5 horizontal lines with four spaces in between, each representing a note on the page from lowest (at the bottom) to highest (at the top). However, notes can expand below and above the staff.

Clefs

There are two main types of "clefs" that musicians use to read music. Below is a picture of the "grand staff".

In the top half of the grand staff, you can see the "treble clef", which consists of higher notes. Many instruments like the piano and the violin use this clef. In the bottom half of the grand staff, you can see the "bass clef", which consists of lower notes. Many instruments like the cello and the tuba use this clef.

Key Signatures

Pictured above is the key of G Major.

Key signatures give information on what notes are in the main scale. A scale is a set of 8 notes that start and end on the same note (ex: C D E F G A B C). The key signature lets the musician know what note the scale will start on.

Key signatures also can include sharps and flats, notes that are slightly higher or slightly lower. The interval a sharp or a flat changes a note by is called a "half step". On a piano, all sharps/flats are the black keys, and all the regular notes, or natural notes, are the white keys.

Time Signatures

Pictured above are three different time signatures.

Time signatures are fractions that indicate how many beats are in a measure, and what length each note will be counted as. The most common time signature is 4/4, which is often referred to as common time. 4/4 essentially means there are 4 beats in a measure and the QUARTER note will get the beat. So, there are four quarter notes in one measure.

Reading the Treble Clef

Many people learn how to read treble clef before bass clef because most common instruments use the treble clef. Each note goes in alphabetical order from A to G, and the first line on the staff of the treble clef is an E. Going up from there, the first space would be F, then the second line would be G, then A, and so on.

There are also notes that extend PAST the staff, on what are called "ledger lines". These are notes that are higher or lower than the staff shows. For example, middle C (named for being in the middle of a piano) is the ledger line directly below the treble clef staff.

Reading the Bass Clef

Next is the bass clef. The first line of the bass clef is a G instead of an E, and it goes in the same alphabetical order treble clef does. One way to remember the notes of bass clef is if you memorize treble clef, every line is moved one line down and every space is moved one space down.

Like the treble clef, the bass clef can have ledger lines that extend past it. In bass clef, middle C is actually the line directly above the staff.

Scales and Types of Key Signatures

There are many types of key signatures, but the main two that people use are major scales and minor scales. Major scales tend to sound happier and more joyful while minor scales tend to sound sadder. Each scale has its own name, using the note the scale starts on and the type of scale it is. For example, C Major is a major scale that starts on the note C. It consists of the notes: C, D, E, F, G, A, and B. It is the only major scale with no sharps or flats (look at key signatures).
